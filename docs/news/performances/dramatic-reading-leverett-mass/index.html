First Project Unspeakables Readings were produced on or around November 22, 2013 to commemorate the 50th anniversary of the assassination of John F. Kennedy

SOA, Columbus, Georgia

A spirited and enthusiastically received reading of a 45-minute version of Project Unspeakable took place on the afternoon of November 22nd at the Columbus Convention Center during the 30th anniversary “School of Americas” (a.k.a. “School of Assassins”) convergence and “vigil” in Columbus, GA, home of Ft. Benning.  (Ft. Benning’s notorious “School” is where the U.S. Army trains Latin American soldiers and police who return to their countries and carry out kidnapping, torture, murder, and coups d’etat.) Readers included a host of veteran activists, including past Nobel Peace Prize nominees Kathy Kelly and John Dear, and Jim Douglass, author of “JFK & the Unspeakable: Why He Died and Why It Matters” (the book that inspired Project Unspeakable).  At the conclusion of the reading, the standing-room-only audience rose to their feet with cheers and applause.

New York City

Recording the reading by NYC’s Theatre Convergence Collective

A SoHo reading was hosted by Convergences Theatre Collective and produced by Elizabeth Stanton & Jeremy Williams. They brought together some amazing talent from the Broadway, Off-Broadway, and prominent independent theatre industry for this reading, including members of Actors Equity. Readers included Erin Layton as “Tenay,” Brennan Pickman-Thoon as “Khrushchev,” Chad Hoeppner as “JFK” and Taavo Smith as “Jim Douglas.” The reading was part of Margot Adler’s piece, Project Unspeakable Asks the Big Questions, which aired on National Public Radio November 30. If you missed listening, here it is again.

Idle Hours, Nashville, Tennessee

Nashville Readers at the Idle Hour

“Our reading was a tremendous success with it’s very own unique cast of characters. After standing around eating shrimp cocktail and turkey roll ups, we drug 3 mismatched tables to the front of the Idle Hour stage and gathered our 12 readers.  Half of them were hillbillies who had never been in the theater.  The other half eager to join in the spirit of the evening.  
Then we launched into Project Unspeakable.  After a little while a black guy came into the Idle Hour, which is unusual, and sat at the bar.  He stayed for the entire reading sitting behind Billy O, a hillbilly, who was trying his best to be Martin Luther King, Jr. Words were misspoken, names were mispronounced but the spirit of the show was intense and magnetic and effected every single person that was there. If we had done it at our house, with 12 of our closest friends, it would not have affected as many people in so many ways…. ahhhh, the mystery of life.”

Seattle, Washington, Keystone UCC.

Portions of the play were read by Friends of Meaningful Movies, a collection of 15 readers from Seattle’s social justice community. The performance was interspersed with historical videos. Watch the full video on YouTube here.

Portland, Oregon

Click here for video from the director and organizers on how they created this reading in two weeks. Veterans For Peace Chapter 72 and Economic Justice/Peace Action Groups of the Unitarian Church put on this living room style reading by military veterans, community and peace activists, radio programmers, students and amateur performers. In addition to the intrinsic benefits of a public reading of Project Unspeakable, they hoped to stir interest from local theatre groups wh0 might consider taking it on and staging dramatic theater productions in the future. Contact Becky Luening, Veterans For Peace Chapter 72, becky.pdx@gmail.com, vfpchapter72.org 

Dramatic Reading in Leverett, MA

Robbie Leppzer photo

Director Ingrid Askew

Full house in Leverett

16 readers captivated the audience in this small town in the Pioneer Valley of Massachusetts on the 50th anniversary of the assassination of JFK.

The evening held special significance for the event’s organizers and Ingrid Askew, the director of the reading. One year ago, James Douglass had read portions of his book at nearby Rowe Conference Center; this sparked the idea for our Project Unspeakables. The reading opened to a standing room only house and closed to a standing ovation.
